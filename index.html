01Indie Games · Eldritch House · Nov 2025 – Present

Shadow Sacrament: The Roots of Evil — Early-Stage Indie Game Research

Role
Sole embedded researcher — full ownership from study design through delivery
Team Context
Small indie studio (~5 people), Eldritch House (Brazil); published by DANGEN Entertainment. No prior research function.

Embedded researcher for Eldritch House, a small indie studio in Brazil (remote), on Shadow Sacrament: The Roots of Evil — a 19th-century survival horror game published by DANGEN Entertainment with an active public demo on Steam. Conducting early-stage discovery and usability research on core gameplay and onboarding, validating design assumptions and focusing limited resources on the highest-impact UX changes before they become too costly to reverse.

One finding from this engagement was reviewed and escalated by the dev team the same day it was delivered. That's the bar embedded research should clear: not findings that get filed, but findings that change something before the next sprint.

Discovery ResearchModerated PlaytestsHypothesis TestingLocalization AuditFrame-by-Frame AnalysisPrioritizationEmbedded ResearcherIndie / Resource-Constrained
What problem were you solving?
Eldritch House needed to know, before committing to redesign work, whether player confusion on their puzzles was a communication failure or a difficulty calibration issue — two problems with completely different solutions. A UI fix and a puzzle redesign are not the same sprint. Map readability was the second priority: could players connect what they saw on the map to where they actually were in the world? Another session extended this into localization: for a Brazilian studio shipping to a PT-BR primary audience, untranslated UI is a credibility and retention risk, not a polish item.
What methods did you use and why?
Session 1 — Moderated playtests with 15-20 representative players per cycle: structured enough to test specific hypotheses, flexible enough to surface unexpected behavior. One finding worth noting: the lantern tutorial gap wasn't visible as a friction point from the outside. The player carried the lantern for 12 minutes without knowing how to use it, navigating dark areas with a tool they couldn't access.

Session 2 — Frame-by-frame localization audit of a 94-minute PT-BR native speaker playthrough. 1,100+ extracted frames reviewed systematically to document untranslated UI surfaces with timestamps and reproduction steps.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
In an indie context, every wrong sprint is a real cost. I consistently synthesized findings into the top 3-5 high-impact changes rather than exhaustive lists. One finding also went through a revision cycle before delivery: initial analysis flagged a missing Resume option as High priority; after dev team clarification, I reframed the finding — the actual risk was Load Game holding the default cursor position, a destructive action that could trigger unintended progress loss. Severity was downgraded to Medium and the recommendation updated accordingly.
What would you do differently?
I would invest earlier in building a lightweight longitudinal panel — a small group of players who could be re-tested as the game evolved — rather than recruiting fresh each cycle. For localization specifically, I would establish a string-tracking protocol from session one so gaps can be cross-referenced against the loc pipeline rather than surfaced only in playthrough review.
Reflection

Working as the sole researcher in an early-stage indie context sharpened my instinct for prioritization — when every sprint costs real development time, you learn quickly which research questions are worth asking and which can wait. The constraint forced a level of clarity in study design I hadn't needed to be as deliberate about in larger team contexts. One finding — a pause menu default cursor issue — was escalated the same day it was delivered, and a post analyzing the methodology behind that finding reached 18,000+ impressions on LinkedIn.

02Independent Study · Disney Speedstorm · May 2024

Disney Speedstorm — Independent UX Research Study

Role
Independent researcher — study design, participant recruitment, facilitation, and synthesis
Context
Independent observational study conducted via PlaytestCloud credits received upon completing Steve Bromley's Games User Research Fundamentals course

An independent observational study on Disney Speedstorm, a live-service mobile racing game by Gameloft, conducted as a practical application of the Games User Research Fundamentals course. Five moderated think-aloud sessions across 4 countries, focused on new player onboarding, progression comprehension, and early-game friction.

Think-Aloud PlaytestingObservational ResearchCross-Session SynthesisBehavioral AnnotationMental Model AnalysisLive-Service GamesNew Player Onboarding
What problem were you solving?
As a practical exercise from the Games User Research Fundamentals course, I wanted to apply end-to-end research methodology to a real live-service game. Disney Speedstorm offered a rich onboarding context — a game with strong brand appeal but a complex progression economy that new players would need to navigate from session one.
What methods did you use and why?
Moderated think-aloud sessions via PlaytestCloud let me observe real new-player behavior without studio access. Five sessions across a diverse participant pool (ages 17-36, 4 countries) gave enough signal to identify cross-participant patterns. I developed a behavioral annotation system using tags (#Usability, #Frustration, #GameDesign, #Monetization) to structure cross-session synthesis.
What did you find?
The most consistent cross-participant finding was confusion around in-game currency and reward systems — flagged independently by 4 of 5 participants regardless of age or gaming experience. Onboarding clarity split clearly by player archetype: experienced kart-racing players found it sufficient; genre-unfamiliar players did not. Controls and visuals received consistent praise across all sessions.
What would you do differently?
With more time I would have recruited specifically for Disney-primary players to better understand the experience gap for that audience. I would also have designed a second evaluative round to test whether specific changes addressed the currency confusion finding — closing the loop between observation and recommendation.
Reflection

Running an independent study on a live production game without studio access sharpened my ability to extract signal from purely observational methods. The most valuable outcome wasn't any single finding but the realization that cross-participant pattern recognition is where the real research judgment lives: one player struggling with currency is noise; four players independently flagging the same system is a finding worth acting on.

03Web3 / Developer UX · Kadena · Oct 2022 – May 2024

Kadena (K-Block) — Blockchain Developer Onboarding

Role
Volunteer UX research contributor — survey research, workflow mapping, and synthesis workshops
Team Context
Collaborative volunteer research team; cross-functional partners across product, engineering, and design

Kadena's blockchain platform had a structural problem: its multi-chain architecture was genuinely novel, but the onboarding documentation assumed mental models developers didn't have. New contributors were encountering the platform's chain indexing and Pact smart contract language without the conceptual scaffolding to make sense of either. The result was early drop-off that looked like a motivation problem but was actually a communication failure.

Working as part of a volunteer research team across product, engineering, and design, we ran a large-scale survey (100+ respondents) to map where developer confidence collapsed during onboarding. The survey wasn't a satisfaction instrument — it was designed to locate the specific moments where the platform's architecture conflicted with user mental models.

Survey ResearchUsability TestingWorkflow MappingSynthesis WorkshopsDeveloper UXMental Model AnalysisBlockchain / Web3
What problem were you solving?
Kadena needed to understand why developer drop-off during onboarding was high despite strong platform capabilities. The hypothesis was documentation quality; the reality was more specific — the documentation was technically accurate but written for someone who already understood what made Kadena different. New developers hit the platform without the mental model needed to interpret what they were reading.
What did you find?
Chain count as cognitive anchor: developers consistently anchored on Kadena's 20-chain architecture as the first point of confusion — not a bug, but an unexplained constraint that made the platform feel arbitrary before they'd written a line of code.

Confidence gap at Pact introduction: developer confidence dropped most sharply at the point where Pact (Kadena's smart contract language) was introduced without a bridge from familiar paradigms. Experienced Solidity developers expected EVM-compatible patterns; general backend developers expected Python-like syntax. Neither expectation held, and documentation didn't acknowledge the gap.
What was the impact?
Follow-up evaluative research after recommendations were implemented measured approximately 25-30% improvement in onboarding comprehension. The K-Block blockchain explorer shipped and is publicly accessible. The core research insight — that developer UX failures are often framing failures, not feature gaps — informed how the team approached subsequent documentation updates.
What would you do differently?
I would push earlier for a longitudinal component: tracking the same cohort of developers through their first 30 days rather than measuring comprehension at a single point. Developer onboarding has a long tail, and a snapshot survey misses the compounding friction that causes drop-off weeks after initial orientation.
Reflection

Developer UX research has different failure modes than consumer research. Developers will tolerate friction that would make a casual user quit — but they'll abandon a platform entirely if it makes them feel like the confusion is their fault. The Kadena finding wasn't that the documentation was incomplete; it was that it was written for someone who already understood what made Kadena different. Closing that gap required reframing the research question from "where do developers get stuck" to "what do developers believe before they encounter this platform, and where do those beliefs break down."
