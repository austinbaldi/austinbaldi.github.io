01Indie Games · Eldritch House · Nov 2025 -- Present

Shadow Sacrament: The Roots of Evil -- Early-Stage Indie Game Research

My Role
Sole embedded researcher — full ownership from study design through delivery
Team Context
Small indie studio (~5 people), Eldritch House (Brazil); published by DANGEN Entertainment. No prior research function.

Embedded researcher for Eldritch House, a small indie studio in Brazil (remote), on Shadow Sacrament: The Roots of Evil — a 19th-century survival horror game published by DANGEN Entertainment with an active public demo on Steam. Conducting early-stage discovery and usability research on core gameplay and onboarding, validating design assumptions and focusing limited resources on the highest-impact UX changes before they become too costly to reverse.

Discovery ResearchModerated PlaytestsHypothesis TestingPrioritizationEmbedded ResearcherIndie / Resource-Constrained
What problem were you solving?
Indie studios cannot afford to build the wrong thing. The core challenge was validating core gameplay mechanics and onboarding before those systems became too expensive to change -- with each test anchored to a specific, high-stakes design decision rather than generating general observations.
What methods did you use and why?
Moderated playtests with 15-20 representative users per cycle were the primary method -- structured enough to test specific hypotheses, flexible enough to surface unexpected behavior. I paired these with targeted hypothesis testing to keep each session anchored to a real decision the team needed to make.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
In an indie context, every wrong sprint is a real cost. I consistently synthesized findings into the top 3-5 high-impact changes rather than exhaustive lists. I also advised directly on scope and development-cost trade-offs -- research was informing what was worth building at all, not just how to build it.
What would you do differently?
I would invest earlier in building a lightweight longitudinal panel — a small group of players who could be re-tested as the game evolved — rather than recruiting fresh each cycle. That continuity would give much better signal on whether changes actually landed over time.
Reflection

Working as the sole researcher in an early-stage indie context sharpened my instinct for prioritization — when every sprint costs real development time, you learn quickly which research questions are worth asking and which can wait. The constraint forced a level of clarity in study design I hadn't needed to be as deliberate about in larger team contexts. If I could revisit one thing, it would be establishing a clearer feedback loop with the dev team earlier — not just delivering findings, but building a shared language for how research connects to specific build decisions.

02Independent Study · Disney Speedstorm · May 2024

Disney Speedstorm -- Independent UX Research Study

My Role
Independent researcher — study design, participant recruitment, facilitation, and synthesis
Context
Independent observational study conducted via PlaytestCloud credits received upon completing Steve Bromley's Games User Research Fundamentals course (gamesuserresearch.com)

An independent observational study on Disney Speedstorm, a live-service mobile racing game by Gameloft, conducted as a practical application of the Games User Research Fundamentals course. Five moderated think-aloud sessions across 4 countries, focused on new player onboarding, progression comprehension, and early-game friction.

Think-Aloud PlaytestingObservational ResearchCross-Session SynthesisBehavioral AnnotationMental Model AnalysisLive-Service GamesNew Player Onboarding
What problem were you solving?
As a practical exercise from the Games User Research Fundamentals course, I wanted to apply end-to-end research methodology to a real live-service game. Disney Speedstorm offered a rich onboarding context -- a game with strong brand appeal but a complex progression economy that new players would need to navigate from session one.
What methods did you use and why?
Moderated think-aloud sessions via PlaytestCloud let me observe real new-player behavior without studio access. Five sessions across a diverse participant pool (ages 17-36, 4 countries) gave enough signal to identify cross-participant patterns. I developed a behavioral annotation system using tags (#Usability, #Frustration, #GameDesign, #Monetization) to structure cross-session synthesis.
What did you find?
The most consistent cross-participant finding was confusion around in-game currency and reward systems -- flagged independently by 4 of 5 participants regardless of age or gaming experience. Onboarding clarity split clearly by player archetype: experienced kart-racing players found it sufficient; genre-unfamiliar players did not. Controls and visuals received consistent praise across all sessions.
What would you do differently?
With more time I would have recruited specifically for Disney-primary players (drawn to the game by the brand rather than the genre) to better understand the experience gap for that audience. I would also have designed a second evaluative round to test whether specific changes addressed the currency confusion finding -- closing the loop between observation and recommendation.
Reflection

Running an independent study on a live production game — without studio access, without internal data, without a team — sharpened my ability to extract signal from purely observational methods. The constraint forced me to be precise about what I could and couldn't claim from the data. The most valuable outcome wasn't any single finding but the realization that cross-participant pattern recognition is where the real research judgment lives: one player struggling with currency is noise; four players independently flagging the same system is a finding worth acting on.

03Web3 / Developer UX · Kadena · Oct 2022 -- May 2024

Kadena (K-Block) -- Blockchain Developer Onboarding

My Role
Volunteer UX research contributor — participated in study design, survey research, workflow mapping, and synthesis workshops
Team Context
Collaborative volunteer research team; cross-functional partners across product, engineering, and design

Volunteer UX researcher contributing to developer onboarding research for Kadena, a blockchain platform. Worked as part of a collaborative research team to identify where the platform's architecture conflicted with user mental models, causing comprehension gaps and early drop-off for developers new to the Kadena ecosystem. The product is live at explorer.chainweb-community.org.

Developer UXUsability TestingSurvey ResearchWorkflow MappingSynthesis WorkshopsOnboarding ResearchBlockchain / Web3Team Leadership
What problem were you solving?
The platform's own architecture -- how it modeled blocks, transactions, and addresses -- conflicted with how users expected a block explorer to work. The research team's goal was pinpointing exactly where those mental model mismatches occurred and surfacing evidence-based recommendations for the onboarding redesign.
What methods did you use and why?
The team used a mixed-methods approach: a large-scale survey study with 100+ respondents to quantify confidence and comprehension gaps at scale, moderated usability testing across experience segments to understand how the same interface created different experiences, and workflow mapping to give teams a structural view of where to restructure onboarding.
How did findings translate into impact?
The team contributed to follow-up evaluative research that measured a ~25-30% improvement in onboarding comprehension after recommendations were implemented. Cross-functional synthesis workshops helped ensure findings didn't get lost between teams with different priorities.
What would you do differently?
I would push earlier for building reusable research assets as an explicit deliverable from the first sprint. I would also advocate for closer integration with analytics data to validate qualitative findings against actual usage patterns -- closing the loop between observed behavior and measured outcomes.
Reflection

Researching a technically complex platform where the product's internal architecture actively conflicts with user mental models was one of the most intellectually demanding challenges I've worked through. It forced me to develop a clearer vocabulary for the gap between system logic and user logic — a distinction I now carry into every engagement. Working as part of a research team on a long-running engagement also taught me how critical cross-phase documentation is: the phases where we had the clearest shared artifacts were the ones where findings actually compounded.

04EdTech / Accessibility · Jul 2023 -- Jan 2026

Children's Dyslexia Reading App -- West Africa EdTech Platform

My Role
Sole researcher across all phases — owned every study from competitive analysis through Phase 3 strategic synthesis
Team Context
Embedded with a distributed open-source development team and nonprofit stakeholders across Nigeria and the UK

Multi-phase UX research supporting the design of an open-source educational app for dyslexic students in West Africa (focus: Nigeria), targeting students, educators, and parents. The project addressed the gap between dyslexia prevalence (~20-33% in Nigeria) and available resources, while combating deep-rooted stigma. The product has not yet launched.

Competitive AnalysisLiterature ReviewUser InterviewsFocus GroupsSurveysUsability TestingProto-PersonasJourney MappingCard SortingAccessibility Research
What problem were you solving?
Over 20% of Nigerians are estimated to have dyslexia, yet 90%+ of teachers lack awareness of it and affordable, culturally relevant tools are virtually nonexistent. The challenge was understanding how dyslexia manifests in a stigma-heavy context, what educators and students actually need, and how to make a product that works within real constraints -- limited internet, Android-only devices, low tech literacy.
What methods did you use and why?
The multi-phase scope required a mixed-methods approach where each method answered a specific gap. Literature review and competitive analysis established the problem space first. Interviews and focus groups with DFN trainers accessed a hard-to-reach educator population. Surveys, card sorting, and lo-fi usability testing each addressed distinct unknowns rather than being applied generically.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
The client needed a scalable, low-cost tool -- open-source, offline-capable, Android-first. When findings pointed to features users wanted (richer animations, voice acting), I helped prioritize which were MVP-critical versus out of scope, keeping the build feasible. Every recommendation was filtered through both lenses: does this serve the child, and can it actually be built?
What would you do differently?
Recruiting dyslexic children and parents proved consistently difficult due to stigma. Earlier community partnerships -- NGOs, schools, parent groups on the ground -- would have unlocked richer primary data sooner. I would also establish a dedicated child-testing protocol from day one, and build more structured phase-to-phase knowledge transfer across a 2.5-year project.
Reflection

This project tested my ability to do meaningful research under conditions that don't exist in most professional contexts — hard-to-reach populations, deep cultural stigma, tight resource constraints, and a 2.5-year timeline that outlasted multiple team changes. What I learned is that longitudinal research is as much a documentation and knowledge management problem as it is a methods problem. The phases where I had the clearest cross-phase artifacts were the ones where findings actually compounded. The phases where I didn't were where we repeated questions we'd already partially answered. I carry that lesson into every engagement now.
