What problem were you solving?
Indie studios cannot afford to build the wrong thing. The core challenge was validating core gameplay mechanics and onboarding before those systems became too expensive to change — with each test anchored to a specific, high-stakes design decision rather than generating general observations. Session 2 extended this into localization: for a Brazilian studio shipping to a PT-BR primary audience, untranslated UI is a credibility and retention risk, not a polish item.
What methods did you use and why?
Session 1 — Moderated playtests with 15-20 representative users per cycle: structured enough to test specific hypotheses, flexible enough to surface unexpected behavior. Paired with targeted hypothesis testing to keep each session anchored to a real decision the team needed to make.

Session 2 — Frame-by-frame localization audit of a 94-minute PT-BR native speaker playthrough. 1,100+ extracted frames reviewed systematically to document untranslated UI surfaces with timestamps and reproduction steps — a different method for a different research question.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
In an indie context, every wrong sprint is a real cost. I consistently synthesized findings into the top 3-5 high-impact changes rather than exhaustive lists. For the localization audit, findings were ranked by player-facing impact — not just presence — so the studio could triage a fix list rather than treat all gaps as equal.
What would you do differently?
I would invest earlier in building a lightweight longitudinal panel — a small group of players who could be re-tested as the game evolved — rather than recruiting fresh each cycle. For localization specifically, I would establish a string-tracking protocol from session one so gaps can be cross-referenced against the loc pipeline rather than surfaced only in playthrough review.
Reflection

Working as the sole researcher in an early-stage indie context sharpened my instinct for prioritization — when every sprint costs real development time, you learn quickly which research questions are worth asking and which can wait. The constraint forced a level of clarity in study design I hadn't needed to be as deliberate about in larger team contexts. One finding from this engagement — a pause menu default cursor issue — was reviewed and escalated by the dev team the same day it was delivered, and a post analyzing the methodology behind that finding reached 18,000+ impressions on LinkedIn, surfacing broader interest in pause menu UX from the games research community. Session 2 expanded the scope into localization: a different method, a different question, and a different kind of deliverable — but the same principle. Research has to be immediately actionable, or it doesn't belong in the sprint.
