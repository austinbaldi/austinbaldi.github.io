01Indie Games · Eldritch House · Nov 2025 -- Present

Eldritch House -- Early-Stage Indie Game Research

Embedded researcher for an indie studio in Brazil (remote). Conducting early-stage discovery and usability research on core gameplay and onboarding -- validating design assumptions and focusing limited resources on the highest-impact UX changes before they become too costly to reverse.

Discovery ResearchModerated PlaytestsHypothesis TestingPrioritizationEmbedded ResearcherIndie / Resource-Constrained
What problem were you solving?
Indie studios cannot afford to build the wrong thing. The core challenge was validating core gameplay mechanics and onboarding before those systems became too expensive to change -- with each test anchored to a specific, high-stakes design decision rather than generating general observations.
What methods did you use and why?
Moderated playtests with 15-20 representative users per cycle were the primary method -- structured enough to test specific hypotheses, flexible enough to surface unexpected behavior. I paired these with targeted hypothesis testing to keep each session anchored to a real decision the team needed to make.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
In an indie context, every wrong sprint is a real cost. I consistently synthesized findings into the top 3-5 high-impact changes rather than exhaustive lists. I also advised directly on scope and development-cost trade-offs -- research was informing what was worth building at all, not just how to build it.
What would you do differently?
I would invest earlier in building a lightweight longitudinal panel -- a small group of players who could be re-tested as the game evolved -- rather than recruiting fresh each cycle. That continuity would give much better signal on whether changes actually landed over time.

02Live-Service Games · Gameloft · Aug 2023 -- Jul 2024

Disney Speedstorm -- Progression & Retention Research

As User Researcher at Gameloft, I led mixed-methods research on Disney Speedstorm's mobile experience, focusing on early-game progression, rewards comprehension, and retention. Research directly shaped how progression messaging and feedback were sequenced across multiple seasonal roadmap decisions.

Mixed-Methods ResearchModerated PlaytestsInterviewsSurveysJourney MappingMental Model AnalysisLive-Service GamesRetention Research
What problem were you solving?
Players were dropping off in the early game. The surface problem was unclear UI, but the deeper issue was a mismatch between what players expected from a Disney-branded kart racer and what the live-service progression system delivered. I needed to identify where that expectation gap was widest and what it was costing in early retention.
What methods did you use and why?
A mixed-methods approach combining playtests, interviews, and surveys captured both behavioral signals and player mental models. I developed a player mental-model-gap framework as a prioritization lens -- mapping the distance between expectation and reality -- which gave the team a durable tool beyond any single study.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
Live-service games run on tight seasonal timelines. I framed every finding around retention risk and delivery feasibility. Reusable insight artifacts (journey maps, expectation-gap analyses, fairness indicators) meant findings did not expire at the end of a sprint -- shortening prioritization cycles by ~20% across subsequent seasons.
What would you do differently?
I would introduce the mental-model-gap framework at the start of the engagement rather than after several rounds of ad hoc research. I would also push earlier for integrating behavioral analytics alongside qualitative work, to distinguish players who were confused from those churning for unrelated reasons.

03Web3 / Developer UX · Kadena · Oct 2022 -- May 2024

Kadena (K-Block) -- Blockchain Developer Onboarding

As UX Researcher at Kadena, I led the research strategy for developer onboarding across a complex blockchain platform -- identifying where system architecture conflicted with user mental models and caused early drop-off. I grew from research contributor into leading strategy, managing apprentice researchers, and coordinating 4 sprints.

Developer UXUsability TestingSurvey ResearchWorkflow MappingSynthesis WorkshopsOnboarding ResearchBlockchain / Web3Team Leadership
What problem were you solving?
The platform's own architecture -- how it modeled blocks, transactions, and addresses -- conflicted with how users expected a block explorer to work. The research problem was pinpointing exactly where those mental model mismatches occurred and quantifying their impact on developer confidence and early drop-off.
What methods did you use and why?
Survey research with 100+ respondents quantified confidence and comprehension gaps at scale. Moderated usability testing across three experience segments showed how the same interface created very different experiences. Workflow mapping covering 80-90% of common use cases gave teams a structural view of where to restructure onboarding flows.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
Growing the developer community was the core business goal -- onboarding friction was a direct business cost. I translated findings into clear onboarding success criteria both product and engineering could act on. Cross-functional synthesis workshops reduced research misinterpretation by ~20%, ensuring findings did not get lost between teams with different priorities.
What would you do differently?
The reusable research assets I built cut setup time by 15-20% on subsequent studies -- I would formalize that from the first sprint and make it an explicit deliverable. I would also push for closer integration with analytics data to validate qualitative findings against actual usage patterns.

04EdTech / Accessibility · Jul 2023 -- Jan 2026

Children's Dyslexia Reading App -- West Africa EdTech Platform

Multi-phase UX research supporting the design of an open-source educational app for dyslexic students in West Africa (focus: Nigeria), targeting students, educators, and parents. The project addressed the gap between dyslexia prevalence (~20-33% in Nigeria) and available resources, while combating deep-rooted stigma. The product has not yet launched.

Competitive AnalysisLiterature ReviewUser InterviewsFocus GroupsSurveysUsability TestingProto-PersonasJourney MappingCard SortingAccessibility Research
What problem were you solving?
Over 20% of Nigerians are estimated to have dyslexia, yet 90%+ of teachers lack awareness of it and affordable, culturally relevant tools are virtually nonexistent. The challenge was understanding how dyslexia manifests in a stigma-heavy context, what educators and students actually need, and how to make a product that works within real constraints -- limited internet, Android-only devices, low tech literacy.
What methods did you use and why?
The multi-phase scope required a mixed-methods approach where each method answered a specific gap. Literature review and competitive analysis established the problem space first. Interviews and focus groups with DFN trainers accessed a hard-to-reach educator population. Surveys, card sorting, and lo-fi usability testing each addressed distinct unknowns rather than being applied generically.
How did you balance business needs with user needs?
The client needed a scalable, low-cost tool -- open-source, offline-capable, Android-first. When findings pointed to features users wanted (richer animations, voice acting), I helped prioritize which were MVP-critical versus out of scope, keeping the build feasible. Every recommendation was filtered through both lenses: does this serve the child, and can it actually be built?
What would you do differently?
Recruiting dyslexic children and parents proved consistently difficult due to stigma. Earlier community partnerships -- NGOs, schools, parent groups on the ground -- would have unlocked richer primary data sooner. I would also establish a dedicated child-testing protocol from day one, and build more structured phase-to-phase knowledge transfer across a 2.5-year project.
